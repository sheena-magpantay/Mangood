HISTORY

    India's religious rituals and folklore are closely linked to the mango. Buddha was given a mango grove so that he may rest in its appreciative shade. The Malayam manna, which the Portuguese called manga when they arrived in Kerala in 1498 to engage in the spice trade, is most likely the source of the name mango, by which the fruit is known in English- and Spanish-speaking nations. The reason behind the tree's introduction into the Western Hemisphere about 1700, when it was planted in Brazil, is likely due to the challenge of transferring seeds, which lose their viability quickly. The tree eventually made its way to the West Indies around 1740.

CULTIVATION

    Mango can grow in both tropical and subtropical climates, from sea level to 1400 m altitude, as long as there is no high humidity, rain, or frost during the flowering time. Mango cultivation works best in areas with a dry summer and sufficient of rainfall. It is best to avoid places with strong winds and cyclones, as these can cause flower and fruit shedding and branch breaking. Mangoes grow on a variety of soil types, from alluvial to laterite, as long as they are well-drained and deep (minimum 6'). It favors soils that are quite acidic (pH 5.5 to 7.5).
